A story about bats helping banana farmers in Malawi

Mostly we hear stories about human–wildlife conflict, but just outside Lilongwe, these bats are improving a banana farm’s yield dramatically. We teamed up with the African Bat Conservation charity to document this story, as well as the dedicated work they are doing to conserve bat populations across Africa.

The bats at Kumbali Lodge in Lilongwe, Malawi are improving banana crop yields in several important ways. Their guano enriches the soil, improves drainage, and speeds up the decomposition process. Bats also feed on insects, significantly reducing the number of pests that damage crops. Finally, by naturally controlling pests, bats reduce the need for chemical pesticides — all while doing this completely free of charge.

This approach offers real hope that similar projects could grow across Malawi, benefiting both smallholder farmers and bat populations alike.

We didn’t know much about bats before this project, and along the way we learned so much, including:

  • Bats are clean animals and rarely spread diseases. Transmission generally only occurs if bats are eaten or in very unusual circumstances where a bat bites. A single adult bat can consume up to 500 insects, including malaria-carrying mosquitoes, in one night.
  • Malawi is home to more than 64 species of bats, making it a true bat hotspot.
  • Of the roughly 1,300 bat species worldwide, only three are blood-feeding bats, and all of them live in Latin America.
  • Very few bats carry rabies, and there is virtually no risk if bats are not handled. Bats have a much lower prevalence of rabies compared to domestic dogs.

Filming in a banana field in the dark, with only head torches for light, was an unforgettable experience. Seeing these bats up close was incredible. We captured the African Bat Conservation team at work — setting up mist nets, collecting data, and safely releasing the bats back into the night.
